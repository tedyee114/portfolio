WIRE STRANDER

CAPSTONE PROJECT | 6 MONTHS

Wire Arc Additive Manufacturing (WAAM) is an experimental type of metal 3D printing that can be leveraged in alloy creation, particularly High Entropy Alloys (HEA's). If feedstock contains multiple metals, they will be can be melted together into custom alloys during deposition. My primary tasks were electrical, power, safety, and UI systems and alloy analysis procedures

 

"A benchtop machine that creates twisted cable feedstock for use with WAAM"

CHALLENGE

By twisting together various metals, alloys can be formed during deposition that cannot be commercially purchased as wire for feedstock. In particular, the near-equal concentrations are good for making High Entropy Alloys (HEA’s). A materials professor posed the project to manufacture large volumes of twisted feedstock and my senior engineering capstone group took it on.

SOLUTION

With my team of 5, we got to practice product design, specifically working to interface with the university’s Fronius/ABB WAAM cell’s feeder, tubing, and plasma torch, where the machine is currently used for alloy research.

I also took on the design of an O&M manual, which is available online on my LinkedIn: https://www.linkedin.com/in/ted-yee/

PROCESS

Personally, I spearheaded electrical, safety, and UI systems.

It was a lot of fun being able to consult with the team about what features we wanted, then allocate enough budget to buy quality components and assemble them in a professional machine rather than just hobby-scale.

ANALYSIS

I was also in charge of alloy analysis procedures, where we looked at the springback that was preventing out early motors from being able to yield the wire into a strand.

02.

VIOLIN

PERSONAL WOODWORKING PROJECT | 5 MONTHS

One time I thought it would be cool to have a violin. Then I saw that violins are expensive. So, I just made one. No template because those are trade secrets, no power tools to make it a challenge.

 

"To create a violin with only hand tools"

Construction Process

Built entirely with hand tools including chisels, hand planes, and scrapers. The process required careful attention to wood grain direction, thickness graduations, and precise joinery without modern conveniences.

Each component was crafted individually: carved spruce top, flamed maple back and sides, ebony fingerboard, and hand-carved scroll - all assembled with traditional hide glue.

Wood Selection & Challenges

Sourced traditional tonewoods: European spruce for the soundboard, flamed maple for back and sides, and ebony for the fingerboard. Each piece was selected for grain quality and acoustic properties.

Working without templates or power tools meant developing techniques for consistent thickness and contour. The biggest challenge was achieving the proper arch graduation for optimal sound projection.

Results & Learning

The finished violin produces a warm, balanced tone suitable for folk and classical music. While not professional-grade, it demonstrates the feasibility of traditional construction methods.

This project taught patience, precision, and respect for traditional craftsmanship. Total cost was under $200 compared to $1000+ for equivalent store-bought instruments.

03.

POLARIS

AUTOMATION AT WRIGHT-PIERCE | 4 MONTHS

One of my main tasks while working at Wright-Pierce Environmental Engineering was to assist municipalities in meeting an EPA mandate about locating lead pipes. As an intern, I led the Polaris project that automated data entry and sorting of public records.

 

"To automate data entry of handwritten public records"

Automation Solution

Developed a Python-based system that used OCR (Optical Character Recognition) and machine learning to digitize handwritten municipal records. The system could process thousands of documents per day with 95% accuracy.

Implemented data validation algorithms and quality control measures to ensure compliance with EPA requirements for lead service line inventories.

EPA Compliance Impact

The EPA mandate required all municipalities to identify and catalog lead service lines by 2024. Manual processing would have taken months; Polaris reduced this to weeks.

Helped over 15 municipalities meet federal deadlines, potentially affecting water safety for hundreds of thousands of residents across New England.

Technical Implementation

Built using Python with libraries including OpenCV for image processing, TensorFlow for handwriting recognition, and Pandas for data management. Created a user-friendly GUI for municipal workers.

The system included error correction workflows, batch processing capabilities, and automated report generation compatible with EPA submission requirements.
